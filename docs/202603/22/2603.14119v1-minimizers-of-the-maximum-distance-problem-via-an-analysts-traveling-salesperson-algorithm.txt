1. Introduction 

Let E ⊂ R2 be a set. Although there are many sets in Euclidean space that cannot be contained in any curve Γ of finite length, for any r > 0, there are many curves of finite length whose closed r-neighborhood N (Γ , r ) := ∪x∈ΓB(x, r ) contains every point in E.The Maximum Distance Problem (MDP) is the problem of finding the shortest such curve: 

(

minimize H1(Γ) 

among curves of finite length Γ ⊂ R2 such that N (Γ , r ) ⊃ E. 

Here and throughout, we define a curve of finite length to be a connected set of finite 1-dimensional Hausdorff measure H1. Not knowing the existence of a minimizer a priori, we define 

Λ( E, r ) := inf {H 1(Γ) : Γ ⊂ R2 is a curve of finite length and N (Γ , r ) ⊃ E}.

However, when E is compact, standard compactness arguments prove the existence of mini-mizers (see [AKV, Theorem 2.15]). The problem was first introduced by Buttazzo, Oudet, and Stepanov [BOS] in the context of the study optimal urban transportation networks in cities. Alvarado, Catalano, Merchán, and Naples [ACMN] show that if E is contained in a compact connected set of finite H1-measure, that is, a curve of finite H1, then the r-maximum dis-tance minimizers converge in both Hausdorff distance and H1-measure to a curve of smallest measure that contains our original set. This is reminiscent of a classical result in harmonic analysis, the so-called Analyst’s Traveling Salesperson Theorem. In the early 1990s, Jones [J]
