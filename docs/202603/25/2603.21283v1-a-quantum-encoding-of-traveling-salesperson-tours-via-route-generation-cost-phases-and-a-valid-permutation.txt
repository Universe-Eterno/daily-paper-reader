Abstract 

We present a compact quantum encoding of the Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP) based on a time-register representation of tours. A candidate route is represented as a se-quence of n city labels over discrete time steps, with one fixed start city and the remaining cities encoded in binary registers. We describe three ingredients of the construction: uniform route generation over the route register, a reversible oracle for marking valid tours, and a phase oracle that encodes the total tour cost. The validity oracle distinguishes permutations of the non-start cities from invalid assignments, while the cost oracle accumulates the con-tribution of the start edge, intermediate transitions, and return edge into a tour-dependent phase. This yields a coherent superposition of candidate routes with feasibility and tour-length information embedded directly in the quantum state. The number of qubits required is O (n log 2(n)) and the circuit depth scales quadratically in n. The encoding is compatible with amplitude amplification or spectral filtering techniques such as the quantum singular value transform (QSVT) or Grover’s algorithm. However, due to the exponentially small fraction of valid tours, the overall complexity remains exponential even when combined with amplitude amplification. 

Keywords: Traveling Salesperson Problem, Traveling Salesman Problem, quantum encoding, phase oracle, permutation oracle, quantum algorithms 

# 1 Introduction 

The Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP) is one of the standard benchmark problems in combi-natorial optimization[1]. Given a weighted graph on n cities, the goal is to find a minimum-cost Hamiltonian cycle that visits each city exactly once and returns to the start. The difficulty of the problem lies in the factorial growth of the number of valid tours with the number of cities. Quantum algorithms provide a natural language for representing exponentially large combi-natorial spaces in superposition. Canonical approaches to combinatorial search include Grover-style amplitude amplification methods[2, 3], quantum backtracking and constraint-satisfaction constructions[4, 5], and more recent spectral and polynomial-filtering frameworks such as quan-tum singular value transformation (QSVT)[6, 7]. 1

Edge-based encodings of the Traveling Salesperson Problem represent candidate solutions through binary variables associated with graph edges, indicating whether a given edge is included in the tour. Such formulations arise naturally in QUBO and Ising-type models used in quantum annealing and variational algorithms. In particular, unconstrained binary formulations of TSP variants explicitly compare edge-based and node-based encodings and analyze their resource requirements, e.g., [8, 9, 10]. In contrast, we adopt a route-based encoding in a quantum circuit model, where candidate tours are represented directly as time-ordered sequences of city labels. This makes the permuta-tion structure explicit and allows feasibility and cost information to be encoded using reversible quantum oracles. # 4 How to use the prepared state 

The prepared state naturally supports subsequent amplification procedures. A first step is to restrict the solution space to the valid subspace using phase-adapted amplitude amplification based on the phase version of the validity oracle [3, 2, 13]. Since the route register contains 

(n−1) n−1 possible label assignments, while only (n−1)! of these correspond to valid permutations, the fraction of valid states is known explicitly as 

(n − 1)! (n − 1) n−1 ∼ √2π(n − 1) e−(n−1) 

by Stirling’s approximation[14]. Consequently, the cost of amplitude amplification scales as 

O (1/√p), so approximately as exp(( n − 1) /2) /(2 π(n−1)) 1/4, growing exponentially in n. Because this success amplitude is known in advance, one may use phase-adapted Grover[13] iterations to rotate exactly into the valid subspace, thereby preparing the normalized equal superposition over all valid tours. Although this procedure provides a quadratic speedup over classical rejection sampling, the exponentially small fraction of valid configurations implies that the overall cost remains exponential in n. Once restricted to the valid sector, one may then apply further transformations that favor shorter tours, for example threshold-based amplitude amplification, polynomial filtering, or QSVT-based spectral methods[3, 6]. # 7 Conclusion 

We presented a compact quantum encoding of TSP tours based on a time-register representation. The route register encodes candidate routes directly, the validity oracle isolates valid Hamiltonian cycles, and the cost oracle attaches the objective function as a coherent phase. The implementa-tion uses O (n log 2(n)) qubits and requires O (n3 log 2(n)) CX gates and O (n3(log 2(n) + log 2(1 /ϵ )) )

T gates. Since gates acting on disjoint time steps can be executed in parallel, the circuit has depth O (n2 log 2(n)) in CX gates and O (n2(log 2(n) + log 2(1 /ϵ )) ) in T gates. The present construction is primarily representational and does not yield a polynomial-time quantum algorithm, as the fraction of valid tours is exponentially small, leading to exponen-tial cost even with amplitude amplification. In addition, the naive cost oracle incurs O (n3)

6controlled operations, resulting in high overall gate complexity despite partial parallelization. Future work should therefore focus on more efficient oracle constructions, for example using re-versible arithmetic or block-encoding techniques, as well as alternative encodings that increase the fraction of valid states. Another promising direction is the use of spectral methods such as QSVT to bias the state toward low-cost tours without explicit feasibility projection.
