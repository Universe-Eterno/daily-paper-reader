# An Analysis of the Coordination Gap between Joint and Modular Learning for Job Shop Scheduling with Transportation Resources 

Moritz Link 1, Jonathan Hoss 1, and Noah Klarmann 1

Abstract — Efficient job-shop scheduling with transportation resources is critical for high-performance manufacturing. With the rise of ”decentralized factories”, multi-agent reinforcement learning has emerged as a promising approach for the combined scheduling of production and transportation tasks. Prior work has largely focused on developing novel cooperative architec-tures while overlooking the question of when joint training is necessary. Joint training denotes the simultaneous training of job and automatic guided vehicle scheduling agents, whereas modular training involves independently training each agent followed by post-hoc integration. In this study, we systematically investigate the conditions under which joint training is essential for optimal performance in the job-shop scheduling problem with transportation resources. Through a rigorous sensitivity analysis of resource scarcity and temporal dominance, we quantify the coordination gap — the performance difference between these two training modalities. In our evaluation, the joint training can produce superior performance compared to the best-performing combinations of dispatching rules and modular training. However, the coordination gap advantage diminishes in bottleneck environments, particularly under se-vere transport and processing constraints. These findings in-dicate that modular training represents a viable alternative in environments where a single scheduling task dominates. Overall, our work provides practical guidance for selecting between training modalities based on environmental conditions, enabling decision-makers to optimize reinforcement learning-based scheduling performance. 

I. INTRODUCTION In today’s competitive industrial landscape, flexibility, cost-efficiency, and productivity are the primary drivers in manufacturing. Optimal scheduling is essential to minimize operational costs, maximize throughput, and ensure effective resource management. With the integration of automatic guided vehicles (AGVs) for material handling, intralogistics scheduling has gained significant relevance. This problem, often defined as job-shop scheduling problem with trans-portation resources (JSSPT), extends the classical job-shop scheduling problem (JSSP) by incorporating transportation constraints [1], [2]. Consequently, the simultaneous opti-mization of production operations and vehicle-based trans-portation is critical for the development of future high-performance manufacturing environments [3].

Various methodologies exist to address the N P -hard JSSP and its variants. While exact optimization methods guarantee optimal solutions, they generally fail to identify feasible solutions within a practical time limit. For this reason, procedures, such as dispatching rules (DRs), and genetic algorithms (GAs), have been widely adopted. DRs facili-tate real-time decision-making with negligible computational overhead but frequently yield sub-optimal results and lack adaptability across diverse problem instances. Conversely, GAs provide superior solution quality but are computation-ally intensive, rendering them frequently unsuitable for real-time scheduling applications [4], [5]. To overcome these limitations, researchers have increas-ingly applied deep reinforcement learning (DRL) to schedul-ing variations, leveraging its adaptability and generalization capabilities in complex decision-making tasks [6]. Existing literature primarily distinguishes between end-to-end and priority dispatching rule (PDR)-based architectures. In PDR-based DRL, the agent’s action space consists of a set of predefined DRs. Despite offering scalability, this approach’s performance is capped by the sub-optimality of the rule set, leading to performance degradation in complex industrial scenarios [7], [8]. To address the distinct requirements of transportation control and operation scheduling, research has shifted from single-agent reinforcement learning (RL) toward multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), focusing on cooperative decentralized architectures [7], [9], [10], [11]. By decomposing responsibilities and action spaces, MARL has demonstrated robust performance in cooperative environ-ments. Despite its potential, MARL is frequently hindered by non-stationarity, increased state-space complexity, partial observability, and the multi-agent credit assignment prob-lem. These challenges result in degraded training stability and inconsistent policy convergence [12]. While specialized training frameworks have been proposed to coordinate agent updates [9], [11], they typically assume centralized control over all agents and the environment. This assumption is often unrealistic in modern ”decentralized factories”, where sub-systems are integrated from multiple vendors. Furthermore, centralized training can be computationally expensive when individual agents or components are replaced. Finally, the incorporation of shared information, such as embeddings, creates interdependencies that restrict the system’s modular-ity and flexibility in dynamic production settings [12], [13]. Given the shift toward multi-agent architectures, this study investigates the conditions under which joint training is essential for JSSPT versus when modular training is suffi-

This research evaluates DRL performance across two modalities: a joint setting, characterized by the concurrent training of job and AGV scheduling agents to foster learned cooperation through MARL, and a modular setting, where agents are trained independently and integrated post-hoc. While prior RL research for JSSPT has focused on proposing cooperative architectures, the question of when joint training is necessary remains largely unexplored. Unlike framework-centric studies, we provide a rigorous sensitivity analysis of the coordination gap, quantifying performance differentials between both training modalities. By evaluating performance across varying resource intensities — the relative scarcity of transportation resources and temporal-dominance ratios, rep-resenting the balance between transportation and processing durations — we identify the specific conditions under which the performance of independent agents diverges significantly from their coordinated counterparts. The main contributions of this study are summarized as follows:  

> •

Introduction of a novel training framework that enables independent agent learning while maintaining compati-bility for joint evaluation.  

> •

A systematic sensitivity analysis of joint versus modular RL training for JSSPT, identifying conditions under which joint training is essential to effectively coordinate both schedulers.  

> •

Characterization of the non-linear impact of resource scarcity and temporal-dominance on the coordination gap, elucidating the functional interdependence of lo-gistics and production scheduling. II. METHODOLOGY

Consider a set of n independent jobs, J =

{J1, J 2, . . . , J n}, and a set of m + 2 machines, 

M = {Ml, M u, M 1, . . . , M m}, where Ml and Mu

m + 1 operations Oj,i , 1 ≤ i ≤ m + 1 . Each operation must be processed on a specific machine μj,i ∈ M for a processing time pj,i > 0. The last operation Oj,m +1

AGVs, with potentially asymmetric transportation times 

t(Mi, M j ) between machines Mi and Mj . The input buffers are infinite and follow a first-in-first-out (FIFO) rule, ensuring that operations arriving at a machine are queued in order without capacity restrictions. Conversely, the output buffers provide infinite capacity without an ordering constraint, enabling an AGV to pick up completed operations in any order. At the beginning, all AGVs start at the load machine. The goal of the JSSPT is to minimize the makespan min Cmax , with Cmax = max j∈J cj,m +1 ,where cj,m +1 denotes the completion time of job j at the unload machine. The completion time is defined as 

cj,i = sj,i + pj,i with the operation’s start time sj,i .The transport completion time cTj,i is determined by the start time sTj,i and the travel time t(·) between machine locations μj,i −1 and μj,i as cTj,i = sTj,i + t(μj,i −1, μ j,i ).To solve the JSSPT, the conditions for the JSSP are enhanced by transportation conditions. For the classic JSSP, the operations have to be processed in sequential order, expressed as Oj, 1 −→ Oj, 2 −→ . . . −→ Oj,m +1 . Additionally, machines can process one operation at a time without preemption. The AGVs are restricted by unit-capacity, with no preemption permitted during transportation. An operation can only be transported after its predecessor completes processing on its machine: sTj,i ≥ cj,i −1, and the processing of the operation starts after transportation: sj,i ≥ cTj,i . In addition, sTj,i depends on the pending tasks of the AGV: 

sTj,i ≥ max( cj,i −1, idle u + t(loc u, μ j,i −1)) , (1) where idle u denotes the time AGV u finishes its pending tasks, and loc u the machine location where u becomes idle.

C. Baseline Solvers and Network Structure 1) Baselines: We use ten DRs for the operation selection and four for the AGV selection, known for their efficient performance [14], [15], [16], [17]. The baseline solvers are constructed by combining each operation-selection rule with each AGV-selection rule. For the operation selection the following DRs are implemented: shortest processing time (SPT), shortest machine processing time (SMPT), longest processing time (LPT), most work remaining (MWR), least work remaining (LWR), flow due date per most work remain-ing (FDD/MWR), most operations remaining (MOR), least operations remaining (LOR), random and first come first serve (FCFS). The AGV rules are: random, shortest pick-up time (SPUT), shortest completion time of in-transport tasks (SCTA), and shortest completion time of in-transport tasks with pick-up time (SCPT).

D. Training Framework 

To evaluate the training dynamics of joint versus mod-ularly trained agents, two distinct training frameworks are implemented within an identical environment to ensure con-sistent conditions. Proximal policy optimization (PPO)[19] is selected as the underlying RL algorithm, following its strong performance in prior literature. To differentiate these training modalities, their respective influences on the MDP formu-lation are described. During joint training, both schedulers are represented by learnable agents, as described previously. Therefore, the π(ao|Sot ) is represented by the GN N and 

π(aagv |Sagv t , a o) by the M LP . The single-agent PPO algo-rithm is enhanced by its multi-agent variation MAPPO [20]. Under this variation, both agents undergo concurrent training with independent parameter updates while being optimized through a joint objective. For the modular training, the job and AGV policy learning are decoupled into two independent training runs. However, to satisfy the MDP definition, the joint action at has to consist of both scheduler decisions. By replacing one policy with a baseline DR, two distinct settings are defined:

π(aagv |Sagv t , a o) = M LP

B. Training Details 

This section details the instance generation procedure for the training phase. To facilitate learning across diverse settings, JSSPT instances of size n × m × k are generated stochastically for the number of jobs n, machines m, and AGVs k. Operation-processing times are sampled from a discrete uniform distribution DU (1 , 100) , with transportation times between machines drawn from the same distribution. According to the instance size (n × m), k is sampled uniformly from DU (3 , n ). The training protocol incorporates diverse instance sizes, which are sampled from a predeter-mined set to facilitate robust learning across different job and machine ratios (6 × 6, 10 × 10 , 15 × 10 , 20 × 5, 30 × 10) .Together with a sampled number of AGVs, this configuration of n × m × k is used to train the agents for one rollout.

E. Analysis of the Coordination Gap and Task Interdepen-dence 

We design a grid experiment using range-based sampling to systematically explore the effect of relative temporal-dominance ( τ ∗) and resource scarcity ( ρ) on solver perfor-mance. The processing times of operations and the trans-portation times of AGVs are independently sampled from discrete consecutive intervals spanning [1 , 100] , defining 10 bins ( [1 , 10] , [11 , 20] , . . . , [91 , 100] ). Each interval combina-tion forms a grid cell, and within each cell, processing and transport times are sampled uniformly from the cor-responding ranges, resulting in systematic coverage of the induced τ ∗ values. For each cell, 20 random instances are generated per configuration listed in Table III, providing a statistically robust dataset across varying levels of resource scarcity and problem scales. The parameters ρ and τ ∗ are integrated into a bivariate parameter space to characterize the interdependence between transport resource allocation and operational duration dynamics. This mapping enables the identification of four distinct operational regimes:  

> •

Underutilized transport ( ρ < 0.5, τ ∗ > 0.0): This regime forms no dominant bottleneck. It is characterized by a restricted number of AGVs, paired with processing duration dominance. This prevents the formation of a dominant bottleneck.  

> •

Process-constrained (ρ > 0.5, τ ∗ > 0.0): In this regime, performance is governed by the underlying job scheduling logic rather than transport constraints. With both abundant resource availability and high transport efficiency, the system is primarily limited by machine processing.  

> •

Transport-constrained ( ρ < 0.5, τ ∗ < 0.0): This regime represents a critical transport bottleneck where vehicle scarcity is exacerbated by transport duration dominance. The system performance is highly sensitive to the trans-port scheduling.  

> •

Resource-saturated ( ρ > 0.5, τ ∗ < 0.0): In contrast to the initial scenario, this regime features a dominance of transportation durations that is mitigated by abun-dant resource availability. This prevents transport from becoming the primary system bottleneck. We utilize these regimes to analyze the interdependency of the two factors and the resulting performance delta between joint and modular training. Figure 2 illustrates the RPI of the joint solver ( J0 A0) in contrast to the best-performing modular solver ( J4 A1). The analysis reveals a distinct hierarchy of solver efficacy across the operational regimes: as illustrated, J0 A0 demonstrates significant performance gains within the resource-saturated and underutilized trans-port regimes, with regime mean RPIs of 4.0% and 3.6% ,respectively. In these regions, the absence of a dominant bot-tleneck allows the joint solver to optimize the schedule more effectively, with peak RPI values reaching up to 8.5% . Con-versely, in the process-constrained and transport-constrained bottleneck regimes, the RPI decreases and solver perfor-mance equalizes. Within the transport-constrained regime, several cells show zero improvement, and one cell even yields a slightly negative value. Consequently, the mean RPIs for these bottleneck regimes decrease to 1.5% and 0.7% ,respectively. In all analyses, a trend of decreasing RPI values is ob-served, corresponding to the increasing dominance of a single scheduling task, while regions with a balanced distribution of tasks exhibit higher RPI values. The negative coefficients for JBN and ABN lead to a decreasing RPI in regions with scheduling task dominance and increase the RPI in balanced regions. The positive coefficient of the balance met-ric yields an increase in RPI with growing balance between tasks. These effects align with the observed behavior in the heatmap (Figure 2), in which the same trend is visible in the regimes. These insights indicate that the coordination gap is driven by the level of functional interdependence between scheduling tasks. The strong balance between transport and processing tasks enables joint training to effectively optimize the coupling between job scheduling and AGV allocation. In contrast, the presence of a dominant bottleneck diminishes the marginal benefits of holistic coordination. With a bot-tleneck, the system’s performance is primarily constrained by a single scheduling task, thereby reducing the relative advantage of joint training over modular approaches. IV. CONCLUSION This study demonstrates the influence of resource avail-ability and temporal dynamics on the performance of joint versus modular training strategies in JSSPT tasks. Overall, joint training of job and AGV scheduling agents outper-forms modular approaches. However, this advantage di-minishes in bottleneck environments, where transport or processing constraints dominate and a hierarchical structure of scheduling tasks exists. These findings have practical implications for the design of RL-based scheduling systems in manufacturing environments. Depending on environmental conditions, decision-makers can select the most appropriate training modality to optimize performance and resource utilization. This work provides a foundation for extending the MARL framework to industrial JSSPT scenarios. Future research will investigate flexible JSSPT with the inclusion of a machine-selection agent, as well as the integration of multiple objectives, including energy consumption, delay minimization, and cost efficiency.
