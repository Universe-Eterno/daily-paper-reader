We introduce RFOX (Rotated-Field Oscillatory eXchange), a parameter-free quantum algorithm for combinatorial optimization. RFOX combines an almost constant non-stoquastic XX catalyst with a weak harmonic ZX counter-diabatic term. Using the Floquet-Magnus expansion, we derive a closed-form effective Hamiltonian whose first-order term retains the full XX driver, while the leading correction consists of a single qubit Y field at high drive frequency. This structure ensures that the instantaneous spectral gap remains essentially flat, independent of both the interpolation parameter and the disorder strength, modulated only by a δ parameter. This behavior stands in stark contrast to the unpredictable gap reductions, or even collapses, exhibited by the X (stoquastic), XX , and X + sXX (non-stoquastic) driver schedules. Extensive noiseless simulations on random-field Ising model (RFIM) instances with 7, 9, and 12 qubits, across three magnetic-field ranges, validate these spectral predictions: RFOX attains near-optimal, and in some cases exact, ground states using up to an order of magnitude fewer Trotter slices. Its performance advantage grows with increasing disorder, as conventional methods slow down near vanishing gaps, whereas RFOX maintains a constant runtime scaling of T ∝ ∆−2min . Hardware experiments on IBM Quantum processors (Eagle r3 and Heron r1, with 12, 15, and 20 physical qubits) reproduce similar performance rankings. These results suggest that fixed-gap, non-stoquastic drivers augmented with analytically derived counter-diabatic terms offer a promising, scalable, and tuning-free route toward quantum optimizers for combinatorial optimization problems.

> I. INTRODUCTION

Inside the quantum computing paradigm, the Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs) have become a cornerstone approach for harnessing near-term quantum devices in the noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) era. Landmark schemes such as the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) [1–3] and the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) [4, 5] exemplify how shallow, parameterized circuits, optimized through a quantum-classical feedback loop, can already tackle demanding problems in quantum chemistry and combinatorial optimization that strain classical resources [6–9]. Beyond these initial demonstrations, a rapidly expanding body of work continues to expand the reach of VQAs, driving progress in ansatz engineering, error-resilient circuit design, and strategies for effective parameter initialization [10–13]. Despite these successes, the hybrid nature of VQAs imposes fundamental bottlenecks. As the system size increases, the classical optimizer encounters barren plateaus, regions where cost-function gradients vanish exponentially, which delay training even in noiseless simulations [14–17]. In real hardware, stochastic gate errors further flatten the optimization landscape and introduce shot noise, overwhelming gradient signals, and making large-scale optimization prohibitively expensive in both circuit executions and classical processing time [18, 19]. To address these limitations, we introduce Rotated-Field Oscillatory eXchange (RFOX) quantum algorithm, an entirely quantum protocol tailored to the random-field Ising model (RFIM) problem. Instead of alternating between quantum circuits and a classical optimizer, RFOX directly encodes the local fields via phase rotations and unifies the cost and mixing dynamics within a single two-qubit gate sequence, U (θ, ϕ ) = RZX (θ) RXX (ϕ), applied to each edge of the RFIM graph. By setting θ, ϕ ∝ ∆t,this construction implements a first-order Trotter step inspired by the quantum adiabatic theorem. To enhance adiabaticity, both gates are modulated by a small harmonic amplitude δ ≪ 1, synchronized with ∆t, generating counter-diabatic corrections while preserving an almost constant non-stoquastic gap. In disordered systems such as the RFIM, where frustrated couplings and random longitudinal fields hinder conventional quantum annealing, introducing a transverse interaction channel X ⊗ X (XX) can accelerate convergence and suppress localization-induced gap closures, outperforming pure transverse field schedules [20–22]. A key design element of RFOX is the combination of a nearly constant non-stoquastic XX driver with a counter-diabatic term of time oscillation Z ⊗ X (ZX). The XX interaction alleviates the stoquastic bottleneck by coupling computational basis states differing by multiple spin flips, thus widening the spectral gap [23–25]. However, a large gap alone does not guarantee adiabatic evolution: when problem fields vary rapidly, residual diabatic transitions can accumulate. The harmonic ZX term serves as the leading-order component of the adiabatic gauge potential, modulated by a phase-shifted sinusoidal envelope that supplies precisely the time derivative required to cancel non-adiabatic couplings, without adding any static cost term that could close the gap. As a result, the combined XX+ZX driver preserves a nearly constant minimum gap while suppressing diabatic leakage, enabling the system to reach approximate, and in some cases exact, ground states at fixed, shallow depth and without classically tuned parameters. Simulations in 7-12 spin RFIM instances show that a circuit depth of p = 100 consistently yields near-optimal, and often exact, ground states, outperforming all other tested methods. We further examine the convergence and scalability of RFOX in randomly generated RFIM instances with 12 to 20 qubits executed on IBM Quantum backends: ibm brisbane , ibm sherbrooke ,and ibm torino . Compared with conventional drivers, either an XX-only catalyst or an X + sXX schedule, RFOX achieves higher ground-state fidelities and faster convergence. By eliminating the classical optimization loop, the protocol reduces operational overhead, improving both the practicality and scalability of quantum optimization on near-term hardware. Section II reviews the theoretical background of the algorithm. Section III provides a formal description of RFOX. Section IV details the generation of RFIM instances and the implementation of these problems on quantum hardware. Section V presents our simulation and experimental results, and Section VI concludes the paper.

> B. Stoquastic vs. non-stoquastic Hamiltonians

To encode the interaction energy term from RFIM in RFOX, we leverage principles from the adiabatic theorem, quantum walks, nonstoquastic Hamiltonians, and counter-diabatic driving. A stoquastic Hamiltonian is one that can be written (on some basis) such that all elements of the off-diagonal matrix are real and non-positive, whereas a non-stoquastic Hamiltonian violates this condition by allowing some (or all) off-diagonal entries to be positive or non-real in any choice of local basis. This sign structure has some important consequences: • Quantum Monte Carlo simulations are free from the negative sign problem, making stoquastic models tractable on classical hardware [28–30]. • In many-body adiabatic algorithms, the Perron–Frobenius theorem guarantees strictly positive ground-state amplitudes for stoquastic Hamiltonians, which suppresses destructive interference and can hinder tunneling through tall, narrow energy barriers [31–33]. • Non-stoquastic Hamiltonians may exhibit ground-state amplitudes with mixed signs, enabling destructive interference paths and enhanced tunneling through rugged cost landscapes [34]. RFOX exploits a non-stoquastic XX interaction [35–37] to allow ground-state amplitudes with mixed signs, thereby enabling stronger quantum interference. In practice, such non-stoquastic drivers can enlarge the minimum adiabatic gap and improve the optimization performance [38, 39]. In fact, non-stoquastic XX drivers have been shown to enhance success probabilities on challenging spin-glass and RFIM instances, sometimes exponentially, relative to standard transverse field protocols [23–25, 40]. After encoding the magnetic field phase, we model the RFIM interaction using both RXX (β) and RZX (γ) operators. > C. Results for Watts-Strogatz RFIM instances

For the Watts–Strogatz RFIM problems (Fig. 7), the results show a consistent pattern where RFOX (blue) outperforms the other drivers across most metrics, though the margin of superiority depends on the problem complexity. In general, the most informative performance indicators are the minimum cost per instance and the cost difference, as these directly measure the effectiveness of an optimization algorithm. For combinatorial optimization problems such as the RFIM, the primary objective is to produce a concrete optimal, or near-optimal, solution. In the case of RFOX, the algorithm can, in certain instances, reach the theoretical minimum and, in others, deliver a close approximation. These outcomes are particularly desirable because the goal is for the optimal solution to emerge as the most frequently observed state at the end of the quantum algorithm, similar in spirit to Grover’s algorithm, without requiring additional classical post-processing. By contrast, relying on the EEV is less practical for larger instances, as the number of measured states can grow substantially, a limitation that becomes even more pronounced on actual NISQ devices. Overall, these results demonstrate that RFOX not only scales effectively to the Erd¨ os-R´ enyi and Watts-Strogatz problems structures but also maintains solution quality under increasingly complex conditions, offering a more balanced and adaptable optimization approach compared to the other tested drivers. 13

> VI. CONCLUSIONS

Our numerical study demonstrates that the RFOX schedule—an always-on, non-stoquastic XX catalyst augmented by a weak, harmonic ZX counter-diabatic kick—consistently outperforms both the X-only and XX drivers, and the commonly used X+sXX catalyst. Across all tested random-field Ising model instances (7, 9, and 12 qubits; three magnetic-field ranges; Erd¨ os–R´ enyi and Watts–Strogatz graphs), RFOX achieves the lowest cost average, the smallest excess energy above the exact optimum, and the shortest average Hamming distance to the ground-state bitstring. These performance gains directly reflect the Floquet-Magnus analysis: the first-order term preserves the full strength of the non-stoquastic XX driver, providing a robust gap floor, while the second-order term introduces only a perturbative Y -field micromotion counter-diabatic correction that refines, rather than narrows, the spectrum. Consequently, the minimum gap remains nearly constant throughout the evolution, whereas comparison schedules exhibit slow starts, gap shrinkage, or even pronounced gap collapse. Since adiabatic runtime scales as ∆−2min , this nearly flat-gap profile enables RFOX to approach the ground state in far fewer Trotter slices, and thus in shallower circuits, than its competitors. Hardware experiments on Eagle-class ( ibm brisbane and ibm sherbrooke ) and Heron-class ( ibm torino ) backends confirm that RFOX’s advantage persists under realistic noise. Although decoherence increases errors across all metrics, RFOX consistently outperforms baseline schedules. This robustness is partly attributable to amplitude-damping noise, which shifts probability weight toward low-energy configurations, a shift that RFOX’s large minimum gap helps preserve. Importantly, RFOX delivers these improvements with shallower circuits as those of the comparator methods and requires no classical parameter optimization. Two further observations arise from our hardware tests. First, on low-density RFIM graphs, conventional non-stoquastic drivers (XX and X+sXX) perform better than they do on the denser graphs studied in simulations. Second, current NISQ devices (e.g., IBM Eagle r3 and Heron r1) accumulate significant errors that severely limit feasible circuit depth. Nonetheless, RFOX’s consistent performance advantage highlights its potential as a scalable, noise-resilient quantum optimizer. Overall, our findings indicate that a near constant-gap, non-stoquastic driver enhanced by a perturbative counter-diabatic term 17 offers an effective and hardware-friendly strategy for quantum optimization. Future research should explore adaptive schemes for the kick amplitude, extend RFOX to a broader class of combinatorial problems, and integrate it with advanced error-mitigation techniques to push beyond the current ∼20-qubit scale. 

VII. FUTURE WORK 

The present study establishes RFOX as a promising, hardware-friendly schedule for random-field Ising models, yet several research directions remain open. • Adaptive counter-diabatic control. • Alternative non-stoquastic drivers. • Broader problem classes. • Analytic complexity bounds. • Qudit and higher-dimensional generalizations. Pursuing these directions will clarify the ultimate scalability of constant-gap, non-stoquastic schedules and help turn RFOX from a proof-of-principle into a practical tool for quantum optimization in the NISQ era and beyond.
