## Abstract 

Traffic signal control (TSC) plays a central role in reducing con-gestion and maintaining urban mobility. This dissertation intro-duces DGLight , a critic-guided reinforcement-learning framework for adapting a pretrained large language model to TSC. DGLight 

first trains a CoLight -based Deep Q-Network critic to estimate traffic-aware action values from structured intersection states, then uses the frozen critic to score candidate language-model actions and optimize the policy with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). The resulting controller maps traffic states to interpretable reasoning traces and signal decisions while learning from dense per-state supervision rather than raw cumulative environment rewards. Experiments on TSC benchmarks covering Jinan and Hangzhou show that DGLight is the strongest overall method among the compared LLM-based controllers, remains competi-tive with strong RL baselines, and transfers well to city datasets not used to fit the critic. Qualitative examples further show that the model’s generated reasoning is interpretable and aligned with the chosen signal phase. The project code is available at https: //github.com/yyccbb/FYP_LLMTSC.

## 1 Introduction 

Rapid urbanization and accelerating motorization have placed un-precedented strain on urban transportation systems worldwide. As cities densify, limited road infrastructure must accommodate ever-growing travel demand, leading to persistent congestion, unreliable travel times, and increased environmental externalities. Inefficient traffic flow not only wastes fuel and economic productivity but also amplifies air pollution and degrades quality of life, particularly in densely populated urban corridors. Traffic signal control (TSC) aims to coordinate phase sequences and durations at signalized intersections to maximize throughput and reduce congestion. Classical fixed-time methods use the Webster formula (Yin, Dridi, and Moudni, 2015) to preset signal timings based on historical traffic data collected from the traffic network at different times. In contrast, the max-pressure control framework (Mercader, Uwayid, and Haddad, 2020) computes a pressure value for each signal phase from the difference between queue lengths on opposing approaches and selects the phase with the highest pres-sure; this strategy can be shown to maximize network throughput. Reinforcement-learning (RL) methods model TSC as a Markov de-cision process in which an agent observes traffic states (for ex-ample, lane queue lengths and delays), chooses an action (phase switch) and receives a reward linked to throughput or delay re-duction. Deep RL further parameterizes the control policy using neural networks, enabling the agent to approximate complex, high-dimensional decision functions. Such methods have demonstrated strong empirical performance across a wide range of simulated traffic scenarios. However, they also introduce notable challenges. In particular, learned policies often exhibit limited generalization, as models trained in one traffic environment exhibit degraded per-formance when deployed in different cities or under unseen con-ditions. Furthermore, the reliance on opaque neural representa-tions reduces interpretability, making it difficult to explain or verify decision-making processes. This lack of transparency poses a bar-rier to real-world adoption, where reliability and accountability are critical. Large Language Models (LLMs) represent a new paradigm in ar-tificial intelligence, combining strong reasoning ability with the capacity to generate interpretable, text-based outputs. These prop-erties make LLMs promising candidates for traffic signal control, where decision transparency and adaptability are highly desirable. By framing control as a reasoning process, LLM-based agents can potentially provide both actionable policies and interpretable expla-nations, bridging the gap between performance and interpretabil-ity. However, this potential is not yet fully realized in practice. Achieving real-time responsiveness typically requires deploying smaller-scale models or mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures with compact expert networks. Without sufficient domain adapta-tion, such models often fail to capture complex traffic dynamics, leading to suboptimal decisions. To this end, I introduce DGLight , a critic-guided reinforcement-learning framework for adapting a pretrained large language model to traffic signal control. The framework combines a Deep Q-Network (DQN) critic with GRPO-based policy optimization so that textual traffic prompts can be scored using dense, traffic-aware action val-ues. In this way, DGLight trains the language model to produce both

## 2 Related Work 

Traffic Signal Control. Early TSC methods such as FixedTime (Yin et al., 2015) rely on predefined cycle lengths and manually designed phase splits for each intersection. Although these approaches are simple and stable in practice, they adapt poorly to time-varying traf-fic demand. MaxPressure (Mercader et al., 2020) improves adaptabil-ity by selecting phases according to the queue imbalance between incoming and outgoing directions, making it more responsive than fixed plans. However, both FixedTime and MaxPressure remain con-strained by handcrafted rules or preset logic, which limits their flexibility under complex and highly dynamic traffic scenarios. Recent progress in reinforcement learning has led to a series of RL-based TSC methods. CoLight (Wei et al., 2019) models network-level cooperation among intersections through graph attention, enabling each signal to coordinate with surrounding intersections instead of acting independently. CoSLight (Ruan et al., 2024) further extends this idea by jointly optimizing signal control and collaborator se-lection, so that each intersection can dynamically decide which neighboring intersections are most useful for coordination under current traffic conditions. MPLight (C. Chen et al., 2020) is a decen-tralized and pressure-aware RL approach designed for large-scale road networks, using parameter sharing to improve scalability and training efficiency. Although these methods achieve strong perfor-mance in per city simulation, RL-based controllers face challenges in generalization and interpretability, hence deployment robustness in real-world environments. To address these limitations, recent studies have begun exploring the integration of large language models (LLMs) into TSC. LLM-Assisted Light (M. Wang et al., 2024) places an LLM at the center of control and augments it with perception and decision-support tools so that it can combine live traffic observations with established TSC methods and remain robust under emergency, roadblock, and sensor-outage scenarios. iLLM-TSC (Pang, M. Wang, Pun, C. S. Chen, and X. Xiong, 2024) instead adopts a hybrid design in which an RL controller first proposes an action and an LLM then verifies or ad-justs that action when observations are degraded or rare events such as packet loss and emergency vehicles fall outside the original RL formulation. LATS (Y. Zhang, P. Li, Zhou, Fan, and Sartoretti, 2026) further explores a teacher-student setting in which an LLM teacher provides rich semantic features during multi-agent RL training, while a smaller student controller learns to emulate those features and is deployed without the LLM at inference time. LLMLight (Lai, Z. Xu, W. Zhang, Liu, and H. Xiong, 2024) moves closer to end-to-end LLM control by adapting its LightGPT backbone through imitation fine-tuning and critic-guided policy refinement so that the model can make direct signal decisions with improved interpretability and cross-scenario performance. Traffic-R1 (Zou et al., 2025) extends this trend with a 3B RL-finetuned foundation model that combines expert-aligned offline training, online self-exploration, and asyn-chronous multi-agent communication to achieve strong zero-shot and out-of-distribution TSC performance. Compared with end-to-end LLM finetuning, LLM+RL hybrids may remain inferior because they still inherit the ceiling and failure modes of the underlying RL controller. Existing end-to-end finetuning methods also have no-table limitations: In LLMLight , the critic-guided policy refinement objective is weaker than modern policy-gradient methods because the reward/advantage magnitude does not directly participate in loss computation. Traffic-R1 , meanwhile, relies on substantial hu-man effort to label the offline finetuning data, and its fixed-horizon cumulative queue-based reward assigns the same coarse mean re-ward to all steps within the horizon. 

RL finetuning of LLMs. RL-based preference alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022) adapts a pretrained LLM using scalar or pairwise feed-back so that the policy is optimized toward preferred responses rather than merely imitating target outputs. Among modern policy optimization methods, TRPO (Schulman, Levine, Moritz, Jordan, and Abbeel, 2015) constrains updates within a trust region to sta-bilize improvement, PPO (Schulman, Wolski, Dhariwal, Radford, and Klimov, 2017) replaces this with a clipped surrogate objective that is simpler and more practical at scale, and GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) further removes the separate value model by computing group-relative advantages over sampled responses, which makes it especially attractive for LLM training. Closely related to these RL-style methods, DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) performs direct pref-erence optimization on chosen–rejected response pairs and avoids explicit reward-model fitting and online rollout collection. This line of work is particularly important because preference-aligned RL can train reasoning ability rather than only answer imitation: through exploration, comparison, and preference-based updates, the model learns to assign higher probability to response trajec-tories that exhibit stronger multi-step reasoning while remaining close to a reference policy through KL regularization, as empha-sized in RLHF-style alignment and recent reasoning-oriented RL studies (Ouyang et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025). Recent systems such as DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025) show that large-scale self-exploration can substantially improve reasoning behavior, making RL preference alignment increasingly relevant for domain-specific agents that must optimize long-horizon objectives.

## 4 Methodology 

This section introduces DGLight , a two-stage reinforcement-learning pipeline for adapting a pretrained large language model to traffic signal control. In the first stage, a CoLight-based DQN critic is trained to estimate action values for signal-phase decisions when provided with structured environment states. In the second stage, the frozen critic scores candidate LLM responses during rollout and the scores are used as the reward signal for GRPO-based policy optimization. Refer to Figure 1 for the pipeline architecture. 

## 4.1 Pretraining the CoLight-Based DQN Critic 

The critic used in DGLight is based on CoLight (Wei et al., 2019), a graph-attentive deep Q-learning model for network-level traffic signal control. CoLight is employed as a pretrained value estima-tor so that the subsequent GRPO stage can query a traffic-aware action-value function rather than rely exclusively on the raw en-vironment reward. Given the current environment state, the critic predicts action values for each intersection and thereby provides a dense estimate of the quality of candidate signal phases under the prevailing traffic situation. The CoLight critic is composed of three main components. The first component is an observation embedding module that trans-forms the local traffic state of each intersection into a latent feature representation. The second component consists of one or more multi-head graph-attention layers, which propagate information across neighboring intersections and produce attention-weighted network representations. The final component is a Q-value predic-tion layer that maps the aggregated features to one scalar value for each admissible signal phase. This architecture combines local state encoding, inter-intersection coordination, and value-based action estimation within a single model. Training follows the standard value-based reinforcement learning procedure. The model interacts with the traffic environment to collect state transitions, actions, rewards, and subsequent states ((𝑠, 𝑎, 𝑟, 𝑠 ′)), where the stepwise reward is defined to be inversely proportional to queue lengths at each intersection. These tran-sitions are stored in replay memory for off-policy updates. The optimization objective minimizes the Bellman error

This strengthens the model’s traffic decision-making capacity while preserving transferability. 

## 4.5 End-to-End DGLight Training Procedure 

Algorithm 1 summarizes the complete DGLight training loop. In practice, the rollout collection and GRPO optimization stages are decoupled. A full rollout episode is first collected with the frozen critic, and the resulting prompt-score records are then used as a separate training set for the subsequent GRPO update. This sepa-ration enables finer-grained optimization, since multiple manual updates can be performed on the same episodic rollout data.

5.1.2 Environment. All methods are evaluated in an environment built on top of CityFlow (Tang et al., 2019), a scalable microscopic traffic simulator widely used in traffic signal control research. For each dataset, the simulator loads the corresponding road-network and traffic-flow configuration and exposes lane-level observations at every control step. In my experiments, each intersection con-nects four lane sections: east, west, north, and south. The control action space therefore contains four signal phases: ETWT (through movements from east and west), ELWL (left turns from east and west), NTST (through movements from north and south), and NLSL 

(left turns from north and south). Right-turn movements are always allowed in the simulation. Each controller selects one signal phase for every controlled intersection, after which the environment ad-vances under a shared signal-switching interval. The green phase duration is fixed at 30 seconds. Each green phase is followed by a 3-second yellow phase and a 2-second all-red interval to ensure safe signal transitions. Unless otherwise stated, all compared methods use the same simulator configuration, decision interval, and evalu-ation protocol so that performance differences can be attributed to the controller rather than the environment setup.

## 5.3 Transferability to Unseen Cities (RQ2) 

The transferability results show that DGLight retains strong perfor-mance on datasets that were not used to fit the DQN critic, namely all three Jinan datasets and Hangzhou-2. More importantly, the mag-nitude of improvement is relatively larger on these unseen datasets than on the datasets most closely aligned with the critic, especially when compared with both the RL baselines and LightGPT . This pattern suggests that the proposed method does not merely adapt to the critic’s judgment, but instead improves the model’s ability to reason about traffic states in a way that generalizes beyond the data seen by the critic.

## 6 Discussion 

In the interim report, I proposed using Group-in-Group Policy Op-timization (GiGPO) for the TSC task, by grouping together near-identical traffic intersection states before computing group-relative advantages. Specifically, the idea was to define a tolerance thresh-old on lane vehicle counts and treat two states as belonging to the same group when the vehicle numbers on every incoming lane differed by no more than that threshold. The motivation was that larger groups of comparable states might improve the stability of advantage estimation during optimization. In the final system, however, this idea was not carried out. The first reason is practical: in the benchmark traffic datasets used in this study, the number of vehicles on each lane is usually small, often fewer than ten. Under this scale, it is difficult to choose a tolerance threshold that is both meaningful and robust. The second reason is methodological and is ultimately more im-portant. The implemented rollout collection method does not store trajectory-specific cumulative rewards in the way GiGPO is de-signed to exploit. Instead, for each observed traffic state, the pipeline records the critic-estimated action values for all admissible signal phases. During the GRPO optimization step, groups can therefore be generated directly by sampling multiple completions for the same prompt and comparing the actions they propose under the stored action-value vector. In other words, the GRPO groups in the final design can be chosen largely according to compute-capacity considerations during optimization rather than by retroactively matching similar states across trajectories. ## 7 Conclusion and Future Work 7.1 Summary of Contributions 

This dissertation presented DGLight , a critic-guided reinforcement-learning framework for adapting a large language model to traffic signal control. The study combined a reimplemented CoLight -based DQN critic with a GRPO-based policy optimization procedure so that a pretrained LLM could be trained using dense traffic-aware action values rather than only short-horizon environment rewards. The resulting pipeline converts structured intersection states into textual prompts, generates interpretable reasoning traces together with signal decisions, and reuses stored per-action value vectors to support efficient offline policy optimization. Empirically, the study showed that DGLight is the strongest overall method among the compared LLM-based controllers on the se-lected CityFlow benchmarks, while remaining competitive with strong RL baselines. The results also suggest that critic-guided GRPO improves transferability beyond the city used to fit the critic and preserves the interpretability benefit of language-based traf-fic control. Taken together, these findings show that value-based traffic knowledge can be transferred into an LLM policy without discarding human-readable reasoning. 

## 7.2 Future Work 

7.2.1 Token-Level Reward Assignment. The current training pipeline assigns a reward at the completion level, which means that all gen-erated tokens are optimized under the same response-level signal. In practice, however, some tokens mainly serve formatting pur-poses, such as section headers, boilerplate phrases, or the fixed decision tag format. These tokens should not necessarily receive the same credit assignment as the tokens that express traffic anal-ysis or the final signal choice. A promising extension is therefore to introduce token-level reward assignment or token masking, so that structurally required formatting tokens are excluded from op-timization or given reduced weight, while informative reasoning tokens and action-selection tokens receive stronger supervision. This may improve training efficiency by aligning the optimization signal more closely with the parts of the output that actually matter for decision quality. 

7.2.2 MLLM-Style Training with a Traffic Situation Encoder. An-other promising direction is to move beyond purely text-serialized traffic states and train an MLLM-style architecture with a dedicated traffic situation encoder. Instead of describing lane counts, queue lengths, and neighboring conditions only through hand-crafted text, a learned encoder could first transform the structured traffic state into a dense embedding that captures the spatial and numer-ical relationships in the intersection. The language model could then condition on this embedding to generate the same text-based reasoning and final signal decision as in the current framework. Such a design may reduce prompt engineering overhead, shorten the effective input length, and preserve more of the original traffic structure, while still retaining the key advantage of interpretable natural-language outputs.
